Rules You Feel, Not Read

May 27, 2026

The article on game design principles covers seven different areas. Goals, mechanics, game flow, balance, feedback, playtesting, and sound design. A lot of these areas sound obvious until you try to build something. The one area that stuck with me the most was the section that talked about game flow. To be exact, when it talked about the idea that a player should never have to think about the controls. The moment that you have to think about the controls, you already lost them.

This is a good concept that reflects perfectly onto the projects that I have been working on this quarter. When I worked on the Every Picture project, I spent a whole lot of time on the hotspot interactions in the interior image. My objective was practically the same as what is described in the article. I wanted to make the next action feel obvious without it needing to be spelt out or explained in some way. If someone has to hunt for, or look for where to click, something has already gone wrong. The interaction should just make sense. I added a pulsing animation to the hotspots recently specifically for this reason.

The playtesting part also stuck out to me. The article is very straightforward with it stating how there is pretty much no formula for fun, and that you have to watch people use the product and just listen. This is something that I have been learning as well. You can just assume that something works but then watch someone use it for literally thirty seconds and then I will see that it just doesn't work. Essentially, the only real fix is to have more eyes on the project.

When to Interrupt — and When Not To

April 22, 2026

After reading Naema Baskanderi's article on modal best practices, it made me rethink a UI pattern I had always taken for granted. Modals originally solved a real problem. They simplified interfaces and preserved screen real estate without navigating users away from their current context. But as Baskanderi points out, designers have leaned on them so heavily that they've started to feel like the pop-up windows of older and more annoying web applications. Users now close them, almost out of instinct.

What I found most useful was the distinction between when to use a modal versus when to reach for something less disruptive, like a toast notification. A modal makes sense when you need the user's full attention, such as a destructive action, or a critical alert, etc. It doesn't make sense for a newsletter signup that fires the moment someone lands on a page.

As someone learning to build interactive, user-focused web projects right now, this reframed how I think about interruption as a design choice. Every modal is essentially asking the user to stop what they're doing. That's a big ask, and it should be saved for a task that deserves this interruption.

Best Practices for Form Design

April 14, 2026

The article talks a lot about many UX concepts, but there are a few things that stand out. Essentially, the idea that the perception of complexity matters as much as actual complexity is something worth thinking about. Users can scan a form before they fill it out, and that creates a lasting first impression which sets the groundwork and shapes whether they even want to try filling it out or not. That connects directly to UI/UX work because you are not just designing fields, but you are handling someone's willingness to continue in the form. The point that the article made about placeholder text being more harmful than helpful also resonated deep to me. The placeholder text disappears the moment someone starts typing, and this puts cognitive load on the user at the worst moment. Labels should be either above or even next to inputs, but not inside them.

A form that exemplifies best practices: Airbnb's booking flow In terms of a real-world form that does this well, Airbnb's booking flow is a great example. It takes a complex reservation process into easy-to-navigate, clearly labeled steps that never shows more than what is needed at one time. Additionally, the inline validation as well is used perfectly, making it a perfect example of a well-built form.

Visual Thinking and the Website That Made Me Rethink Navigation

May 5, 2026

One of the first things we learn in design is that form follows function, but what happens when form is the function? That question kept coming up for me when I looked at Bruno Simon's portfolio at bruno-simon. It's a fully 3D environment where you drive a tiny toy car around to navigate the site. No nav bar, no scroll, no menus. Just a car, a world.

What interested me most is how much it reframes what "reading" a website even means. In the NYT article on close reading and visual thinking, the idea is that a single image rewards patient, layered attention, since you notice more the longer you look. Bruno Simon's site works the same way, except the image is interactive and moves with you. The visual information isn't presented to you, you discover it by moving through it. That shift from just simply viewing something to exploring it is how meaning becomes valuable.

The obvious drawback is the site's load time. This is because it is more of a technical showcase rather than a practical template. Not every site should be a video game. But as a proof of concept, it makes a compelling argument: that interaction itself can be a visual language, and that the way you navigate something shapes what you take away from it. For me, it was a reminder that the most memorable design experiences aren't always the ones that communicate the most clearly. Sometimes, the most memorable experiences are the ones that make you feel something that you never anticipated.
